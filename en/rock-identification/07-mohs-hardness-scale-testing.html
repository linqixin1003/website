Mohs Hardness Scale Testing

Practical Hardness Testing in the Field
The Mohs hardness scale, developed by Friedrich Mohs in 1812, remains the standard method for testing mineral hardness. This relative scale from 1 to 10 provides one of the most reliable diagnostic properties for mineral identification and is essential for every field geologist.

Scratched by copper penny. Effervesces with acid. Most common carbonate mineral.

4 - Fluorite

Perfect octahedral cleavage. Often fluorescent under UV light. Wide color range.

5 - Apatite

Essential component of teeth and bones. Important phosphate mineral.

6 - Orthoclase

7 - Quartz

No cleavage, conchoidal fracture. Most common mineral in Earth's crust.

8 - Topaz

Perfect basal cleavage. Important gemstone. High refractive index.

9 - Corundum

Ruby and sapphire varieties. Second hardest natural material. No cleavage.

10 - Diamond

Hardest natural material. Perfect octahedral cleavage. Ultimate abrasive.

üîß Field Testing Tools
Professional field testing requires a systematic set of hardness testing tools:

üíÖ Fingernail (2.5)

Tests very soft minerals like talc and gypsum. Clean fingernail essential for accurate results.

ü™ô Copper Penny (3.5)

Distinguishes calcite and similar carbonates. Use clean, uncoated pennies for best results.

üî™ Steel Knife (5.5)

Most useful field tool. Separates soft from hard minerals. Use quality steel blade.

ü™ü Glass Plate (5.5)

Alternative to steel knife. Window glass or microscope slide. Shows clear scratches.

üîß Steel File (6.5)

Tests moderately hard minerals. Hardened steel file gives consistent results.

üíé Hardness Pencils

Professional set with exact hardness values. Most accurate field method available.

üéØ Testing Technique
Proper technique is essential for accurate hardness determination:
üî¨ Professional Method
  1. Select fresh, unweathered surface
  2. Apply firm, controlled pressure
  3. Draw testing tool across mineral surface
  4. Examine for actual scratch (not powder residue)
  5. Test both directions - mineral scratching tool vs. tool scratching mineral
  6. Clean surfaces between tests
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Errors
  • Mistaking powder residue for scratches
  • Testing on weathered or altered surfaces
  • Using insufficient pressure
  • Not testing reciprocal hardness
  • Ignoring crystal face variations
üìä Hardness Ranges
Understanding hardness ranges helps with quick field identification:

üü¢ Soft Minerals (1-3)

Scratched by fingernail or penny. Include talc, gypsum, calcite. Often feel greasy or chalky.

üü° Medium Minerals (4-6)

Scratched by knife, scratch glass. Include fluorite, apatite, feldspar. Most common range.

üî¥ Hard Minerals (7+)

Scratch steel, very durable. Include quartz, garnet, corundum. Excellent abrasives.

üîç Diagnostic Applications
Hardness testing provides immediate diagnostic information:

üí∞ Gold vs. Pyrite

Gold: Hardness 2.5-3, malleable. Pyrite: Hardness 6-6.5, brittle. Definitive test.

üíé Diamond vs. Glass

Diamond: Hardness 10, scratches all materials. Glass: Hardness ~5.5, scratched by steel.

üßÇ Halite vs. Sylvite

Both taste salty, but halite: 2.5, sylvite: 2. Subtle but measurable difference.

‚öóÔ∏è Atomic Basis of Hardness
Understanding why minerals have different hardnesses helps predict and interpret results:

üîó Bond Strength

Covalent bonds (diamond) are stronger than ionic bonds (halite). Metallic bonds vary widely.

üèóÔ∏è Crystal Structure

3D frameworks (quartz) are harder than layer structures (mica). Packing density matters.

‚öõÔ∏è Atomic Size

Smaller atoms pack more tightly, increasing hardness. Compare diamond (carbon) vs. graphite.

üìê Directional Hardness
Some minerals show different hardness in different directions:

üó°Ô∏è Kyanite

Hardness 5 parallel to length, 7 perpendicular to length. Classic example of directional hardness.

üíé Diamond

Slight directional variation. Some faces harder than others. Important for cutting.

üî∑ Calcite

Hardness varies on different crystal faces. Cleavage surfaces may seem softer.

üè≠ Industrial Applications
Hardness testing has crucial industrial applications:

üîß Abrasives

Hardness determines abrasive use. Diamond (10) for cutting, garnet (7-8) for sandpaper.

üíé Gemstones

Hardness affects durability and value. Emerald (7.5-8) softer than ruby (9).

üèóÔ∏è Construction

Aggregate hardness affects concrete strength. Quartz sand preferred over soft limestone.

üî¨ Advanced Hardness Testing
Laboratory methods provide precise quantitative hardness measurements:

üî® Vickers Hardness

Diamond pyramid indenter with measured load. Provides numerical hardness values.

üìè Knoop Hardness

Elongated diamond indenter. Better for brittle materials and thin sections.

üéØ Nanoindentation

Ultra-small scale testing. Can measure hardness of individual mineral grains.

Scale Limitations: The Mohs scale is logarithmic, not linear. The difference between 9 and 10 (corundum to diamond) is much greater than between 1 and 2 (talc to gypsum).
üéì Building Testing Skills
Developing hardness testing expertise requires systematic practice:
üìà Skill Development
  • Practice with known mineral specimens first
  • Learn to distinguish scratches from powder residue
  • Test multiple areas on each specimen
  • Build muscle memory for proper pressure
  • Keep testing tools clean and sharp
‚ö†Ô∏è Safety and Ethics
üõ°Ô∏è Safe Testing Practices
  • Always obtain permission before testing specimens
  • Avoid testing valuable or museum specimens
  • Test inconspicuous areas when possible
  • Be aware that testing damages specimens
  • Use minimal force necessary for clear results
