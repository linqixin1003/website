Pressure & Temperature Effects

How Extreme Conditions Transform Rocks
Pressure and temperature are fundamental forces that drive rock transformation in Earth's interior. Understanding how these conditions affect rock formation and modification is crucial for interpreting metamorphic processes, magma generation, and the evolution of Earth's crust and mantle.
üå°Ô∏è Temperature Effects
Temperature increases with depth in Earth's interior and has profound effects on rock behavior:

Geothermal Gradient

Temperature increases ~25-30¬∞C per kilometer depth. Varies with tectonic setting and heat flow.

Melting Effects

High temperatures cause partial or complete melting, generating magmas with different compositions.

Recrystallization

Heat promotes mineral recrystallization without melting, creating metamorphic textures.

Chemical Reactions

Temperature controls reaction rates and mineral stability, driving metamorphic changes.

‚öñÔ∏è Pressure Effects
Pressure in Earth's interior comes from multiple sources and affects rock properties:

Lithostatic Pressure

Confining pressure from overlying rock weight. Increases uniformly with depth (~270 bars/km).

Directed Pressure

Non-uniform stress from tectonic forces. Creates foliation and lineation in metamorphic rocks.

Fluid Pressure

Pressure from pore fluids. Can approach lithostatic pressure in some environments.

üîÑ Combined P-T Effects
Pressure and temperature work together to control rock behavior and mineral stability:
  • Phase Transitions: P-T conditions determine which mineral phases are stable
  • Reaction Kinetics: Higher P-T accelerates chemical reactions
  • Deformation Mechanisms: P-T controls how rocks respond to stress
  • Fluid Behavior: P-T affects fluid composition and mobility
üìä P-T Diagrams
Phase diagrams show mineral stability fields as functions of pressure and temperature, essential tools for understanding metamorphic processes.
üèîÔ∏è Metamorphic Facies
Metamorphic facies represent specific P-T conditions and associated mineral assemblages:

Low P-T Facies

Zeolite and prehnite-pumpellyite facies. Shallow burial, low-grade metamorphism.

Medium P-T Facies

Greenschist and amphibolite facies. Regional metamorphism in mountain belts.

High P-T Facies

Granulite and eclogite facies. Deep crustal and mantle conditions.

üåã Melting Relationships
P-T conditions control when and how rocks melt to form magmas:
  • Solidus: Temperature at which melting begins for given pressure
  • Liquidus: Temperature at which melting is complete
  • Partial Melting: Incomplete melting produces magmas with different compositions
  • Pressure Effects: Higher pressure generally increases melting temperature
Key Concept: The presence of water dramatically lowers melting temperatures, explaining why subduction zones are sites of extensive magma generation.
üíé High-Pressure Minerals
Extreme pressure conditions create unique mineral phases:

Diamond

High-pressure form of carbon, stable at >4.5 GPa. Indicator of mantle conditions.

Coesite

High-pressure form of quartz. Evidence of meteorite impact or deep subduction.

Stishovite

Ultra-high-pressure silica polymorph. Forms only under extreme shock conditions.

Laboratory experiments simulate natural P-T conditions:
  • Piston-Cylinder: Medium P-T conditions (up to 4 GPa, 2000¬∞C)
  • Multi-Anvil Press: High P-T conditions (up to 30 GPa, 2500¬∞C)
  • Diamond Anvil Cell: Ultra-high pressures (up to 500 GPa)
  • Shock Wave Experiments: Extreme P-T conditions of impact events
üåç Natural P-T Environments
Different geological settings provide various P-T conditions:

Shallow Crust

Low P-T: <0.5 GPa, <400¬∞C. Contact metamorphism and hydrothermal alteration.

Deep Crust

Medium P-T: 0.5-1.5 GPa, 400-800¬∞C. Regional metamorphism and anatexis.

Upper Mantle

High P-T: 1.5-15 GPa, 800-1500¬∞C. Mantle metasomatism and melting.

Subduction Zones

High P, variable T: Complex P-T paths with blueschist and eclogite formation.

üìà P-T-Time Paths
Rocks follow complex P-T paths through time during geological processes:
  • Prograde Path: Increasing P-T during burial and heating
  • Peak Conditions: Maximum P-T reached during metamorphism
  • Retrograde Path: Decreasing P-T during exhumation and cooling
  • Clockwise vs. Counterclockwise: Different tectonic settings create different path shapes
üéØ Thermobarometry
Geologists use mineral compositions and assemblages to estimate the P-T conditions of rock formation using calibrated thermometers and barometers.

P-T conditions affect the rates of geological processes:
  • Diffusion Rates: Higher T increases atomic diffusion in minerals
  • Reaction Rates: Both P and T affect chemical reaction kinetics
  • Deformation Rates: P-T controls creep mechanisms in rocks
  • Fluid Flow: P-T affects fluid viscosity and mobility

  • Petroleum Systems: P-T affects hydrocarbon generation and migration
