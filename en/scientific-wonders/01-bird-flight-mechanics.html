Basic Principles of Lift Generation
The core of bird flight lies in the generation of lift. According to Bernoulli's principle, when air flows over the upper surface of a bird's wing, the airflow accelerates due to the curved design of the wing, resulting in lower pressureüí®. Meanwhile, the airflow beneath the wing is relatively slower with higher pressure, and this pressure difference creates an upward lift.
However, lift generation doesn't solely depend on wing shape. Birds can effectively control the amount of lift by adjusting the angle of attack (the angle between the wing and the horizontal plane)üìê. When the angle of attack increases, lift increases, but drag also increases, requiring birds to constantly make precise adjustments during flight.
Complex Mechanisms of Wing Flapping
The flapping of birds' wings is not a simple up-and-down motion, but a complex three-dimensional movement. During the upstroke phase, the wings move upward and backward, with the wings partially folding to reduce drag.

Adaptability of Different Flight Modes
Different birds have evolved various flight modes according to their living environments and foraging needs. Raptors have broad wings suitable for gliding and soaringü¶Ö. Birds like swallows have slender wings, ideal for rapid maneuvering flight and catching insects in the air.
Water birds typically have narrow, elongated wings, a design beneficial for long-distance flight and taking off and landing on water surfaces. Although penguins cannot fly, their "wings" function like fish fins in water, demonstrating similar principles of fluid dynamicsüêß.
