Tool-Using Abilities of Crows
Corvids are among the smartest species in the animal kingdom, capable of making and using complex tools to solve problemsüîß. New Caledonian crows can bend leaves into hook-shaped tools to extract insects from tree holes, a behavior that requires high levels of spatial imagination and causal reasoning.

Crows can solve multi-step logical problems, with intelligence levels comparable to 7-year-old children.
Even more amazingly, crows possess a "theory of mind," allowing them to understand the intentions and knowledge states of other individualsüí≠. They adjust their strategies based on other crows' behaviors and may even deliberately mislead competitors.
Parrots' Language Learning Talent
Parrots not only mimic human language, but more importantly, they can understand the meaning of vocabulary and engage in meaningful communicationü¶ú. The famous African Grey Parrot Alex mastered over 150 English words, could identify colors, shapes, quantities, and even express emotional needs.
Research shows that parrots' language learning ability is closely related to their social complexity. In the wild, parrot groups have complex social structures and communication systemsüë•. These social demands have driven the evolution of their language abilities.
Mathematical Concepts in Birds
Many birds possess basic mathematical concepts, capable of simple counting and quantity comparisonsüî¢. Pigeons can learn to sort by numerical size, while crows can understand the concept of "zero" - abilities that are extremely rare in the animal kingdom.
More interestingly, some birds also demonstrate geometric intuition. They can recognize symmetry, understand angular relationships, and even master basic spatial geometric conceptsüìê. These abilities are significant for their foraging, nest-building, and navigation.
Emotions and Self-Awareness
Birds not only possess advanced cognitive abilities but also demonstrate complex emotional responses. They can feel sad when losing a mate and excited when successfully solving a problemüò¢. Some studies even suggest that certain birds may possess self-awareness.

Uniqueness of Brain Structure
The structure of bird brains is drastically different from mammals, yet they produce similar cognitive functions. Birds lack a neocortex, but other regions of their forebrain are highly developedüß¨. This phenomenon of "convergent evolution" suggests that intelligence can be achieved through different neural structures.
Bird brains have extremely high neuron density; although small in volume, they are highly efficient in information processing. This compact and efficient design provides new insights for understanding the nature of intelligence and the development of artificial intelligence‚ö°.
