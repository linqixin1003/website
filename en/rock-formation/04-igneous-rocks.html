Igneous Rock Types & Classification

Understanding Rocks Born from Fire
Igneous rocks form from the cooling and solidification of molten material (magma or lava). They represent the primary rocks of Earth's crust and provide crucial insights into the planet's interior processes. Understanding their classification helps geologists interpret Earth's history and processes.
üî• Formation Environments
Igneous rocks form in two main environments, each producing distinct rock types:

Intrusive (Plutonic) Rocks

Form when magma cools slowly beneath Earth's surface, allowing large crystals to develop. Examples: granite, gabbro, diorite.

Extrusive (Volcanic) Rocks

Form when lava cools rapidly at Earth's surface, creating fine-grained or glassy textures. Examples: basalt, rhyolite, andesite.

Felsic Rocks (65-75% SiO‚ÇÇ)

Intermediate Rocks (55-65% SiO‚ÇÇ)

Medium-colored rocks with intermediate composition. Intrusive: diorite. Extrusive: andesite.

Mafic Rocks (45-55% SiO‚ÇÇ)

Dark-colored, iron and magnesium-rich rocks. Intrusive: gabbro. Extrusive: basalt.

Ultramafic Rocks (<45% SiO‚ÇÇ)

Very dark, dense rocks rich in olivine and pyroxene. Example: peridotite (mantle rock).

üîç Textural Classification
Texture refers to the size, shape, and arrangement of crystals in igneous rocks:
  • Phaneritic: Coarse-grained texture with visible crystals (>1mm)
  • Aphanitic: Fine-grained texture with crystals too small to see
  • Porphyritic: Mixed texture with large crystals in fine-grained matrix
  • Glassy: No crystal structure, rapid cooling prevented crystallization
  • Vesicular: Contains holes (vesicles) from trapped gas bubbles
  • Pyroclastic: Composed of fragments from explosive eruptions
üî¨ Texture Tells the Story
Crystal size directly relates to cooling rate: slow cooling = large crystals (intrusive), fast cooling = small crystals or glass (extrusive).
ü™® Common Igneous Rocks
Here are the most important igneous rocks and their characteristics:

Granite

Coarse-grained, felsic intrusive rock. Light-colored with visible quartz, feldspar, and mica. Common in continental crust.

Basalt

Fine-grained, mafic extrusive rock. Dark-colored, rich in pyroxene and plagioclase. Most common volcanic rock.

Gabbro

Coarse-grained, mafic intrusive rock. Dark-colored with visible pyroxene and plagioclase. Oceanic crust composition.

Rhyolite

Fine-grained, felsic extrusive rock. Light-colored, often contains quartz phenocrysts. Explosive eruption product.

üíé Special Igneous Rocks
Some igneous rocks have unique characteristics or formation conditions:

Obsidian

Natural volcanic glass formed by extremely rapid cooling. Sharp edges make it useful for tools.

Pumice

Highly vesicular volcanic rock that can float on water due to trapped gas bubbles.

Pegmatite

Extremely coarse-grained igneous rock with crystals larger than 2.5cm. Often contains rare minerals.

Tuff

Rock composed of consolidated volcanic ash and pyroclastic fragments.

This series explains why mafic rocks crystallize at higher temperatures and felsic rocks at lower temperatures.
üåç Tectonic Settings
Different igneous rocks form in specific tectonic environments:
  • Mid-Ocean Ridges: Basalt and gabbro from decompression melting
  • Subduction Zones: Andesite and diorite from partial melting
  • Continental Hotspots: Rhyolite and granite from crustal melting
  • Oceanic Hotspots: Basalt from mantle plume activity
üî¨ Identification Techniques
Geologists use several methods to identify igneous rocks:
  • Hand Specimen: Color, texture, mineral content, density
  • Thin Section: Microscopic mineral identification and texture
  • Chemical Analysis: Major and trace element composition
  • Field Relations: Geological context and associations
üéØ Quick ID Tips
Light colors + coarse texture = granite; Dark colors + fine texture = basalt; Mixed crystal sizes = porphyry; Glassy appearance = obsidian or volcanic glass.
üí∞ Economic Importance
Igneous rocks have significant economic value:
  • Construction: Granite for countertops, basalt for road aggregate
  • Ore Deposits: Many metal deposits associated with igneous intrusions
  • Gemstones: Diamonds in kimberlite, emeralds in pegmatites
  • Geothermal Energy: Heat from cooling igneous bodies
