Common Garden Pests and How to Recognize Their Damage

Effective pest management begins with accurate identification, and often the first clue to a pest problem is the damage it causes rather than the insect itself. Chewing Damage

Chewing Damage: Holes, Notches, and Skeletonization

Insects with chewing mouthparts (beetles, caterpillars, grasshoppers) create visible holes and missing tissue:

  • Caterpillars: Create irregular holes in leaves, often starting from leaf edges. Some species (like cabbage loopers) create "windows" by eating the leaf surface while leaving the opposite side intact. Look for frass (droppings) near damage.
  • Beetles: Japanese beetles and Colorado potato beetles create skeletonized leaves (only veins remain) or large, irregular holes. Flea beetles create small, round holes, giving leaves a "shot-hole" appearance.
  • Slugs and Snails: Create large, irregular holes with smooth edges, often leaving a slimy trail. Damage is most common in moist, shady areas.

Piercing-Sucking Damage: Discoloration and Distortion

Insects that pierce and suck plant fluids (aphids, whiteflies, scale insects, spider mites) cause different symptoms:

  • Aphids: Cause yellowing, curling, or distortion of leaves. They excrete honeydew, a sticky substance that promotes sooty mold growth. Look for clusters of small, soft-bodied insects on new growth or undersides of leaves.
  • Spider Mites: Cause stippling (tiny yellow or white dots) on leaves, which may appear bronzed or scorched. Fine webbing may be visible, especially in dry conditions.
  • Scale Insects: Appear as small, immobile bumps on stems or leaves. They cause yellowing, leaf drop, and stunted growth. Heavy infestations can kill branches or entire plants.
  • Whiteflies: Cause yellowing and wilting. When disturbed, clouds of tiny white insects fly up from plants.

Mining and Boring Damage: Hidden but Destructive

Some insects feed inside plant tissues:

  • Leaf Miners: Create winding trails or blotches between leaf surfaces. The damage appears as white or brown lines or patches within the leaf tissue.
  • Borers: Tunnel into stems, branches, or trunks, causing wilting, dieback, or plant death. Look for entry holes, sawdust-like frass, or oozing sap.
  • Root Feeders: Grubs (beetle larvae) and root maggots feed on roots, causing wilting, stunting, or plant death. Damage may not be visible until plants are severely affected.

Distinguishing Pest Damage from Other Problems

Not all plant damage is caused by insects:

  • Disease: Fungal and bacterial diseases often cause spots, wilting, or discoloration that can be mistaken for insect damage. Look for patterns and symptoms specific to diseases.
  • Environmental Stress: Drought, nutrient deficiencies, or temperature extremes can cause symptoms similar to pest damage.
  • Beneficial Insects: Some damage (like holes from beneficial beetles or caterpillars that become butterflies) may be acceptable or even desirable in diverse ecosystems.
Accurate identification requires examining both the damage and the plant's overall health and context.

Field Note: Systematic Damage Assessment

When assessing plant damage, examine both the upper and lower leaf surfaces, stems, and roots if possible. Look for the actual insects, their eggs, or signs like frass, webbing, or honeydew. Note the pattern and extent of damage, and consider environmental conditions. This systematic approach helps identify the cause and determine appropriate management strategies.

Recognizing common garden pest damage patterns is the first step toward effective, targeted pest management. By understanding how different insects feed and the symptoms they cause, gardeners can identify problems early, choose appropriate interventions, and distinguish between actual pest issues and other plant problems, leading to more successful and sustainable garden management.
