How to Identify Rocks and Minerals

Identifying rocks and minerals is a fundamental skill for any rock collector. While some identification requires laboratory analysis, many common rocks and minerals can be identified in the field using simple observation and basic tests.

Basic Identification Steps

Follow these steps when trying to identify a rock or mineral specimen:

  1. Observe the overall appearance - Note the color, texture, and any visible crystals or layers.
  2. Check the hardness - Use the Mohs hardness scale to determine relative hardness.
  3. Examine the streak - Test the color of the mineral powder by scratching it on a streak plate.
  4. Check for cleavage or fracture - Observe how the rock or mineral breaks.
  5. Note any special properties - Look for magnetism, fluorescence, or reaction to acid.
  6. Compare with reference materials - Use field guides or online resources for comparison.

Identifying the Three Rock Types

Understanding the three main rock types can help narrow down identification:

  • Igneous Rocks - Formed from molten magma. Look for interlocking crystals, glassy texture, or vesicles (gas bubbles). Examples: granite, basalt, obsidian.
  • Sedimentary Rocks - Formed from deposited sediments. Look for layers, fossils, or visible grains. Examples: sandstone, limestone, shale.
  • Metamorphic Rocks - Formed from existing rocks changed by heat and pressure. Look for foliation, recrystallization, or new mineral growth. Examples: marble, slate, gneiss.

Common Mineral Identification

Some common minerals and their key identification features:

  • Quartz - Hard (7 on Mohs scale), glassy luster, conchoidal fracture.
  • Feldspar - Hard (6-6.5), two directions of cleavage, often pink or white.
  • Mica - Soft (2.5-3), perfect cleavage in one direction, thin sheets.
  • Calcite - Soft (3), reacts with acid, rhombohedral cleavage.
  • Pyrite - Brassy yellow color, cubic crystals, hard (6-6.5), nickname "fool's gold".
  • Magnetite - Black or dark brown, magnetic, hard (5.5-6.5).

Simple Field Tests

These simple tests can help with identification in the field:

  • Hardness Test - Use common objects to test hardness (fingernail ~2.5, penny ~3, steel nail ~5.5, glass ~6.5).
  • Streak Test - Scratch the mineral on an unglazed porcelain plate to see the streak color.
  • Acid Test - Apply a drop of dilute hydrochloric acid to test for carbonate minerals (calcite will fizz).
  • Magnet Test - Use a magnet to test for magnetic minerals like magnetite or hematite.
  • UV Light Test - Shine a UV light on the specimen to check for fluorescence (some minerals glow).
